What students learn

Spectra Explorer is designed so students learn by doing, not by reading. The game's puzzles are built directly on real astronomy and chemistry concepts:

1
Identify stellar spectral classes (OBAFGKM Harvard system) by reading absorption and emission line patterns
2
Distinguish between absorption spectra (dark lines on rainbow) and emission spectra (bright lines on black)
3
Interpret Doppler shift — determine radial velocity direction from spectral line displacement
4
Apply Kepler's Third Law (T²=a³/M) to determine whether a planet's orbit falls within the habitable zone
5
Identify elements from pure emission spectra drawn from the periodic table
6
Assess planetary habitability using atmospheric pressure, surface temperature, magnetic field, and orbital stability
7
Predict stellar evolution endpoints (white dwarf, neutron star, black hole) from stellar mass

Difficulty scales automatically. New players see full explanations and absorption-only spectra. Advanced players encounter emission, binary composite, and partial-window spectra with formula-based classification.

Welcome to Spectra Explorer

Reading Spectra

Starlight spread into a rainbow reveals dark gaps where elements in the star's atmosphere absorbed specific colours. These gaps are called absorption lines — each element always absorbs at the same wavelengths, making the pattern a unique fingerprint.

Think of it like a barcode. Hydrogen always absorbs at 656nm (red), 486nm (blue-green), 434nm (violet). Calcium always absorbs at 393nm and 397nm. Match the barcode, identify the star.

What to look for

  • Count the lines — hot stars (O, B) have few lines. Cool stars (M) have so many the spectrum looks nearly black.
  • Where they cluster — lines bunched on the left (blue end) = hot. Spread across the right (red end) = cool.
  • Spacing — evenly spaced bold lines = A-type hydrogen. Tight, irregular clusters = cooler metal-rich stars.

Emission vs absorption

Some puzzles show an emission spectrum — bright coloured lines on black instead of dark lines on rainbow. The line positions are the same; only the format is inverted. Match the positions, not the colours.

Star Classes — OBAFGKM

Stars are classified by surface temperature from hottest (O) to coolest (M). Temperature determines which absorption lines appear — hotter stars strip electrons from atoms, changing what light gets absorbed. The mnemonic: Oh Be A Fine Girl/Guy, Kiss Me.

In the game's Act III, you already know the temperature, luminosity and radius from earlier steps. Use temperature first — it narrows you to 1–2 classes immediately. The spectral description then confirms it.

Quick reference

O
30,000–50,000 K · Blue-white · Very few, widely-spaced lines. Rarest and hottest. Uninhabitable — lethal UV radiation. Explodes as a supernova after just a few million years.
B
10,000–30,000 K · Blue-white · Few lines, some helium. Still extremely hot. Hydrogen lines present but not dominant. Examples: Rigel, Regulus.
A
7,500–10,000 K · White · Strong, evenly-spaced hydrogen lines. Easiest class to identify — the Balmer series (656, 486, 434, 410nm) is strongest here. Examples: Sirius, Vega.
F
6,000–7,500 K · Yellow-white · Hydrogen weakens, calcium and iron appear. More lines than A-type, slightly more crowded. Examples: Procyon, Polaris.
G
5,200–6,000 K · Yellow · Dense, rich spectrum. Many lines across the middle. Our Sun's class. Best habitability — stable, long-lived. Examples: Sun, Tau Ceti.
K
3,700–5,200 K · Orange · Very dense spectrum with molecular bands beginning. Excellent habitability — lives even longer than G stars. Examples: Epsilon Eridani.
M
2,400–3,700 K · Red · Extremely dense lines — nearly solid black in the red half. Most common star type. TiO molecular bands are the key identifier. Examples: Proxima Centauri, TRAPPIST-1.
The game does not show temperature ranges on the classification buttons — you must use the data above to decide. Temperature is your fastest shortcut; the spectral description is your confirmation.

▸ Why these thresholds? (science detail)

Pressure (0.3–3.0 atm) — Earth is 1.0 atm. Too thin and water evaporates or freezes. Too thick and you get a crushing greenhouse. Mars is 0.006 atm — far too low.

Temperature (−20 to +70°C) — liquid water range with a margin for extremophiles. Wider than 0–100°C because some microbes survive at −20°C in brine, and up to +120°C in hydrothermal vents at pressure.

Magnetic field (≥ 8 μT) — Earth's field is ~50 μT. Without it, stellar wind strips the atmosphere over geological time. Mars lost its field ~4 billion years ago and its atmosphere soon after.

Orbital stability (≥ 55) — a highly elliptical orbit causes extreme seasonal swings. A planet passing from boiling to frozen each year cannot maintain liquid water long enough for life to take hold.

▸ How Doppler shift works

When a star moves toward Earth its light waves compress — all its spectral lines shift slightly left (toward shorter/bluer wavelengths). Moving away stretches the waves — lines shift right (longer/redder). The puzzle shows two strips: the observed spectrum and a reference. Find the same distinctive line in both and check which direction it moved.

Easiest line to track: hydrogen H-alpha at 656nm (the rightmost prominent line). If it appears slightly left of its reference position, the star is approaching. Slightly right — receding.
▸ How the Kepler puzzle works

The formula is a = ∛(T² × M) where T is the planet's orbital period in years, M is the stellar mass, and a is the distance from the star in AU. The in-game calculator walks you through all three steps — just enter your answer in the input field.

Worked example: T=1yr, M=1 → 1² × 1 = 1 → ∛1 = 1.0 AU — Earth's orbit. Inside the Sun's habitable zone (0.95–1.37 AU). ✓

The in-game calculator automatically fills in Steps 1 and 2 for your first 10 correct answers, then progressively removes scaffolding — after 10 correct it leaves only Step 1 pre-filled, and after 20 correct all steps are blank. This is intentional: the goal is for you to eventually work through the formula independently.

▸ How element identification works

Each element emits light at specific wavelengths — its spectral fingerprint. The puzzle shows an emission pattern and asks which element produced it. Early on, the four answer choices each show their own emission strip so you can compare visually. After 5 correct answers the strips disappear — you'll need to recall the patterns from memory or use the 📖 Glossary's Element Spectra tab.

▸ How stellar evolution works

A star's mass determines its entire life and death. The puzzle gives you the star's mass in solar masses (M☉) and asks what it will eventually become:

  • Below 0.5 M☉ → Black Dwarf (fades out slowly)
  • 0.5–8 M☉ → White Dwarf (our Sun's fate)
  • 8–20 M☉ → Neutron Star (supernova remnant)
  • Above 20 M☉ → Black Hole (complete collapse)
▸ Electron configuration (appears after correct Element ID)

After correctly identifying an element, a follow-up question asks you to identify its ground-state electron configuration. This closes the loop between the spectrum you matched visually and the atomic physics that produced it.

The valence electrons — the outermost shell — are what produce the emission lines you see. When a photon from the star excites one of those electrons to a higher energy level, the specific wavelength emitted as it falls back is exactly the line you matched.

Three difficulty tiers: choose the correct config from four options (0–4 correct) → only valence shell shown (5–9 correct) → all steps blank (10+ correct). The ⚛️ Configurations tab in the 📖 Glossary is a full reference for all 86 elements.

▸ Survey Sprint — timed challenge mode

Survey Sprint is an optional timed mode accessible from the ⬡ Sector Jump screen. Classify 10 stars correctly within 10 minutes. An orange timer bar appears in the HUD showing time remaining.

A speed bonus of up to +1,000 points is awarded on completion — the faster you finish, the more points. The sprint counts correct emission matches only; other puzzle types continue normally. Hit Quit on the timer bar to end early without penalty.

▸ Mystery objects — anomalous classifications

Every 8–14 stars, you may encounter an object that doesn't fit any OBAFGKM class. These appear with a coloured badge and distinctive prefix:

  • BD prefix — Brown Dwarf. Too cool for hydrogen fusion. Classify as Anomalous.
  • TT prefix — T Tauri star. Pre-main-sequence protostar still forming. Classify as Anomalous.
  • WD prefix — Polluted White Dwarf. Heavy metal lines that shouldn't exist at this temperature. Classify as Anomalous.

In Act III, an ? Anomalous Object button appears alongside the normal OBAFGKM choices. Selecting it correctly awards +175 pts. The explanation shown is real astrophysics — these objects genuinely don't fit the classification system, and noticing that is what real scientists do.

▸ Special events — variable stars, flares, binaries

Every 6–9 correct scans, a special event star appears. You'll hear a three-note chime and see a coloured badge below the star name with a science explanation. Three event types:

  • 🔮 Variable Star — a pulsating star whose brightness varies cyclically. The puzzle mechanic is identical — classify from the line pattern. The event note explains the science of Cepheid and RR Lyrae variables.
  • ☀️ Stellar Flare — an energetic magnetic reconnection event on an M-dwarf, causing anomalous blue brightening. The science note explains why flares matter for planetary habitability.
  • ⚡ Spectroscopic Binary — two stars in a close orbit producing a composite spectrum. The science note explains how binary stars are detected from Doppler line splitting.

Special events don't change how you play Act I — they add context and science. The mission lore below the star name is grounded in real observational astronomy.

Research Mode

Research Mode unlocks after discovering your first star in The Heliopause. It adds two new puzzle variants to the main game loop — the same four acts, but Act III and the bonus puzzles become significantly more demanding.

Research Mode is a prestige layer — it doesn't replace the main game. All ten sectors remain playable with their standard puzzles. Research Mode variants appear when replaying sectors after the unlock.
▸ HR Diagram Placement (replaces classification buttons)

Instead of clicking O B A F G K M, you place the star on an interactive Hertzsprung-Russell diagram with five clickable regions: Supergiant, Giant, Main Sequence, Subgiant, and White Dwarf.

The key insight this puzzle teaches: temperature alone doesn't classify a star. A G-type temperature (5,750K) could belong to a main sequence dwarf, a subgiant, a giant, or even a white dwarf. Luminosity and radius together with temperature determine where on the diagram a star belongs — and therefore its luminosity class (Ia, II, III, IV, V).

The diagram shows your target star as a yellow dot. Clicking a wrong region reveals why that region is ruled out by the data — part of the learning is understanding what each region of the HR diagram actually represents.
▸ Spectroscopic Binary Mass (new mandatory puzzle for composite spectra)

When a composite binary spectrum is detected, both stars' absorption lines are visible shifting back and forth in opposite directions as they orbit. The velocity amplitude of each star is inversely proportional to its mass:

M₁ / M₂ = V₂ / V₁

The more massive star moves slower — it has a smaller orbit around the centre of mass. The puzzle shows a radial velocity graph with two sinusoidal curves (one per star) and gives you both amplitudes. You must identify which star is more massive and estimate the mass ratio.

This is exactly how stellar masses are measured in practice. Spica (α Virginis), one of the brightest stars in the sky, was first determined to be a binary system in 1890 using this technique.
▸ Research Log (replaces Mission Report)

In Research Mode, the session summary becomes a formatted Research Log showing all derived quantities alongside measured parameters. For each star analysed it records:

  • Spectral type and luminosity class (from HR diagram placement)
  • Binary mass ratio if a composite spectrum was detected
  • Lithium age estimate and Li/Fe line ratio (if Heliopause sector)
  • Orbital period and planet classification from radial velocity curve
  • ISM tracer lines identified (Ca II K, Na I D)
  • Electron configuration for each identified element

The Research Log can be printed or copied — it's formatted like a brief observational report suitable for submission as a lab exercise at introductory university level.
